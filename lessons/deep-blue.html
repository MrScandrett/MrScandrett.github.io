AI Origins · Lesson 10

IBM Deep Blue: The Chess Machine

Brute force conquered chess. In 1997, a machine finally defeated the world champion.

The Match That Changed Everything

Two contests. Six games each. One question: could a computer think its way to victory?

The 1996 Match — Kasparov Wins

  • ResultGarry Kasparov defeated Deep Blue 4–2 in Philadelphia.
  • NotableGame 1 was the first time a world chess champion lost to a computer under standard tournament conditions.
  • Human edgeKasparov adapted his strategy mid-match, exploiting weaknesses in Deep Blue's opening book and endgame play.
  • IBM responseIBM rebuilt the hardware with faster chips and refined the evaluation function for the rematch.

The 1997 Rematch — Deep Blue Wins

  • ResultDeep Blue defeated Kasparov 3½–2½ in New York — the first time a computer defeated a reigning world champion in a full match.
  • Game 2 shockDeep Blue played a positional sacrifice that stunned Kasparov, who later said it looked like a move only a human could find. He resigned a position that was later shown to be drawable.
  • ControversyKasparov requested printouts of Deep Blue's thinking but IBM declined. He claimed a human operator may have intervened in Game 2. IBM denied it.
  • RetirementIBM retired Deep Blue immediately after the match and never played again.

How Deep Blue Actually Worked

  • SearchMinimax with alpha-beta pruning — the same algorithm you studied in Lesson 05 and 06.
  • Speed200 million chess positions per second using 480 custom VLSI chess chips running in parallel.
  • DepthTypically searched 6–8 moves ahead, with extensions up to 20+ moves in forced lines (check sequences, captures).
  • EvaluationA hand-tuned function with ~8,000 features scoring material, piece mobility, king safety, pawn structure, and more — all encoded by grandmaster consultants.
  • Opening bookA database of grandmaster games for the first ~25 moves — Deep Blue often played the opening from memory, not search.
  • No learningDeep Blue could not learn or update between games. Every move was the result of programmed rules plus search — pure brute force intelligence.

Play Chess vs. Stockfish

You play White. The AI plays Black using Stockfish — the open-source successor to Deep Blue's brute-force lineage, and the strongest chess engine ever built.

Stockfish vs. Deep Blue

  • Same ideaStockfish uses minimax + alpha-beta + extensions — the same core approach as Deep Blue.
  • Much fasterStockfish evaluates ~70 million positions/sec in JavaScript — 200M+/sec in native C++.
  • ScopeNo castling or en passant in this classroom build.
  • PromotionPawns auto-promote to Queen on the back rank.

    Learning Goals

    • Goal 1Explain how minimax + alpha-beta pruning enables a computer to search millions of chess positions per second.
    • Goal 2Describe the role of an evaluation function and explain why having one makes chess tractable for AI.
    • Goal 3Contrast how increasing search depth affects move quality, and identify the tradeoff with computation time.
    • Goal 4Articulate why the same brute-force approach that worked for chess completely fails for Go.

    Key Concepts

    • MinimaxSearch all moves to a fixed depth; assume each player always picks their best option.
    • Alpha-BetaPrune branches that can't possibly improve the best move found so far — up to 10× speedup with good move ordering.
    • Evaluation fnA fast heuristic that scores a board position without searching to the end — material count, mobility, king safety.
    • Search depthEach additional ply (half-move) multiplies the search tree by the branching factor (~35 in chess). Deep Blue used hardware to go deeper than any prior system.
    • QuiescenceDeep Blue extended search in "loud" positions (captures, checks) to avoid evaluating mid-exchange positions — important for accuracy.

    Lesson Outputs

    Play at least one full game, then discuss with your group.

    Discussion After your game, answer these questions ① Deep Blue used 480 custom chips. Stockfish runs in your browser. What does that say about how fast hardware has improved?
    ② How did Kasparov adapt his strategy between 1996 and 1997 — and why wasn't it enough the second time?
    ③ Deep Blue couldn't learn between games. Does that mean it wasn't intelligent? What is intelligence?
