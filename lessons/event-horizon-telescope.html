Event Horizon Telescope

The Event Horizon Telescope is an international collaboration that linked radio observatories across Earth and produced the first image of a black hole. This lesson models the big idea: more synchronized stations, longer baselines, sharper detail.

Why It Worked

Three ideas to keep in mind

  • One dish is not enough to resolve a black hole shadow from Earth.
  • Large separations create long baselines, which improve angular resolution.
  • Without synchronized clocks, the recordings cannot be aligned and combined.

Concept 01

Very-long-baseline interferometry

The EHT did not build a literal giant dish. It combined observations from telescopes spread across Earth so the array behaved like a much larger instrument.

Concept 02

Atomic clocks and correlation

Each site timestamped its data extremely precisely. Later, correlators aligned those recordings so the wave patterns could be combined instead of becoming meaningless noise.

Concept 03

What the image shows

The famous ring is glowing material around the black hole. The darker center is the shadow region connected to the event horizon, where gravity bends light and traps anything crossing the boundary.

Concept 04

Why the first image mattered

The first EHT image gave direct visual evidence of black hole-scale structure and showed that a global collaboration could use Earth itself as a telescope platform.
