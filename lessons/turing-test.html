AI Foundations · Lesson 04

The Turing Test

How Alan Turing reframed machine intelligence and shaped the foundations of modern computing.

Biography: Alan Turing

  • WhoAlan Turing (1912-1954), British mathematician, logician, and computer science pioneer.
  • Computer ScienceDefined the Turing machine (1936), proving that one universal machine could execute any computable algorithm.
  • Systems ImpactHis wartime codebreaking and machine design work accelerated practical computing and algorithmic engineering.
  • Robotics LinkHis ideas about machine rules, feedback, and adaptive behavior helped frame how autonomous agents and robots can make decisions.

Transition into This Lesson Plan

Turing's 1950 question, "Can machines think?", becomes this lesson's structure. You will move from the original imitation game to classroom testing, critique, and reflection.

  • Step 1Study the imitation game model and how the test works.
  • Step 2Compare strengths and criticisms of behavior-based AI evaluation.
  • Step 3Run a mini classroom Turing Test with human and AI responses.
  • Step 4Write a claim-evidence reflection on whether the test still matters today.

Continue to the lesson activities below.

The Turing Test (Imitation Game)

A judge chats with two hidden participants: one human and one machine.

How It Works

  • Step 1The judge can only text-chat with each participant.
  • Step 2The human and machine both try to sound convincingly human.
  • Step 3The judge decides which one is the machine.
  • ResultIf judges cannot reliably tell, the machine "passes" the test.

Strengths and Criticisms

  • StrengthBehavior-based and easy to run as an experiment.
  • CriticismGood conversation does not prove understanding or consciousness.
  • Modern viewUseful as one benchmark, but not the only measure of intelligence.
  • Class takeawayDefine what kind of intelligence you are testing before evaluating an AI.

Classroom Activity: Run a Mini Turing Test

Compare human and AI responses using the same prompt set.

  1. Round 1
    Write three prompts

    Use one factual question, one creative prompt, and one ethical scenario.

  2. Round 2
    Collect anonymous answers

    Mix one human response and one AI response for each prompt.

  3. Round 3
    Judge each answer

    Vote on which answer seems human and explain your reasoning.

  4. Round 4
    Reflect

    Decide whether your rubric measured intelligence, style mimicry, or both.

Lesson Outputs

Reflection Write a short claim-evidence paragraph Prompt: Is the Turing Test still useful for evaluating modern AI systems? Use at least two pieces of evidence from class discussion.
