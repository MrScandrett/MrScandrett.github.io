AI Origins · Lesson 10

Deep Blue to AlphaGo: The Go Challenge

Chess fell to brute force. Go needed something entirely new.

The Two-Machine Story

Two games. Two eras. Two completely different AI breakthroughs.

IBM Deep Blue (1997)

  • What it didDefeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov 3½–2½ in a 6-game match.
  • How it workedMinimax search + alpha-beta pruning, evaluating up to 200 million positions per second on custom hardware.
  • Board size8×8 — 64 squares, branching factor ~35 moves per turn.
  • Why chess was solvableMaterial values (queen = 9, rook = 5…) gave a reliable evaluation function. Deeper search = stronger play.

Why Go Broke Everything

  • Board size19×19 — 361 intersections. More possible game positions than there are atoms in the observable universe (~10¹⁷⁰ vs 10⁸⁰).
  • Branching factor~200 legal moves per turn (vs ~35 in chess). Minimax runs out of time before reaching a useful depth.
  • No easy evaluationThere is no "capture the king." Territory scoring requires strategic intuition — a beginner cannot tell who is winning by counting pieces.
  • Life & deathA group is alive if it can form two "eyes" (enclosed empty spaces). Reading life and death requires seeing many moves ahead in an impossibly large tree.

AlphaGo (DeepMind, 2016)

  • The breakthroughDefeated 9-dan professional Lee Sedol 4–1 in March 2016 — a milestone most experts thought was a decade away.
  • Policy networkA deep neural network trained on millions of expert games learned which moves humans consider, cutting the branching factor dramatically.
  • Value networkA second neural network estimated the probability of winning from any position — replacing the impossible task of searching to the end.
  • Monte Carlo Tree SearchMCTS guided the search: explore promising branches deeper, abandon weak ones faster — similar (in spirit) to the AI in this lesson.

Go in 30 Seconds

  • PlaceClick any open intersection to place your Black stone.
  • CaptureSurround all of an opponent's group on every side — those stones are removed.
  • TerritoryEmpty intersections fully surrounded by your stones count as your territory at the end.
  • PassHit Pass when you have no useful moves. Two passes in a row end the game.
  • KomiWhite gets 6.5 bonus points (komi) for going second.

Learning Goals

  • Goal 1Explain why Go's branching factor makes minimax impractical without a better search strategy.
  • Goal 2Describe the role of Monte Carlo random playouts in estimating move quality.
  • Goal 3Distinguish Deep Blue's brute-force approach from AlphaGo's neural network + MCTS hybrid.
  • Goal 4Identify at least one way AlphaGo Zero improved on AlphaGo without human game data.

Key Concepts

  • LibertyAn empty intersection directly adjacent to a stone or group. Zero liberties = captured.
  • Ko ruleYou cannot return the board to its exact previous position — prevents infinite loops.
  • MCTSMonte Carlo Tree Search: explore → simulate random games → back-propagate win/loss to guide future exploration.
  • KomiPoints given to White to compensate for Black's first-move advantage.

Lesson Outputs

Play at least one full game, then discuss with your group.

Discussion After your game, answer these questions ① How does the AI's move quality compare to Deep Blue's chess play, and why?
② If you could add one improvement to make this AI stronger, what would it be?
③ AlphaGo Zero learned with zero human games. What does that suggest about AI and intuition? Extension Implement UCB1 selection in MCTS Replace random candidate sampling with Upper Confidence Bound scoring to balance exploration and exploitation.
