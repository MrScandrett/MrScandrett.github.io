AI Origins · Lesson 01

Before Computers: Myths, Logic, and AI Imagination

Long before modern software, people imagined artificial minds through myth, philosophy, machinery, fraud, and science fiction.

Myths of Artificial Life

Early stories treated intelligence as something humans could build, summon, or animate.

Talos (Ancient Greece)

  • IdeaA bronze guardian that patrolled Crete like an autonomous security machine.
  • AI lensOne of the earliest stories of an artificial agent with a specific programmed role.

Pygmalion's Statue and the Golem

  • PygmalionA crafted figure brought to life, linking human creativity with artificial life.
  • GolemA man-made being animated by words and rules; powerful but risky when control fails.

Philosophers Who Framed AI Concepts

These thinkers introduced core ideas still used in computation and AI today.

  1. 6th c. BCE
    Anaximander

    Among the earliest known thinkers to model the universe mechanically rather than mythologically.

  2. 5th c. BCE
    Democritus

    Argued reality is built from atoms and rules, a precursor to rule-based and computational worldviews.

  3. 4th c. BCE
    Aristotle

    Developed formal logic, foundational for symbolic AI, inference, and proof systems.

  4. 13th c.
    Ramon Llull

    Built rotating logical wheels to combine concepts and generate knowledge mechanically.

  5. 17th c.
    Rene Descartes

    Suggested animals and bodies can be understood as biological machines; paired with "I think, therefore I am."

Fraud, Fear, and Fiction

Public ideas about AI were shaped as much by storytelling as by science.

The Mechanical Turk (18th Century)

  • ClaimA chess-playing automaton that seemed to think on its own.
  • RealityA hidden human operator controlled it from inside the cabinet.
  • LessonEarly AI snake oil: impressive demos can hide human labor and mislead audiences.

HAL 9000 and I, Robot

  • HAL 9000In 2001: A Space Odyssey, HAL shaped the public fear that advanced AI may become opaque and dangerous.
  • I, RobotIsaac Asimov's linked stories explore robots governed by the Three Laws, showing how safety rules can still create ethical conflicts and unexpected outcomes.
  • Big takeawaySociety has always asked not only "Can we build intelligence?" but also "Can we trust it?"
